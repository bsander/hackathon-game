## Ideate — 2026-04-02

- When a game's core loop feels flat, adding mechanics on top (steal phases, combo counters, disruption actions) doesn't fix the underlying problem — it decorates it. The team repeatedly rejected incremental additions until the conversation shifted to replacing the loop entirely. Lesson: if the loop is wrong, change the loop.
- "Evolve beyond RPS" was the wrong framing. The actual gap was directed player-to-player interaction — nothing a player did felt aimed at a person. Reframing as "add directedness" was more precise but still pointed toward bolting features onto the existing game. The breakthrough came from asking "what core loop creates social tension?" instead of "what do we add?"
- UNO's social energy comes from four properties: targeted (hits a specific person), legible (everyone sees it), reversible (you can come back), and socially deniable ("I had to play it"). In the cauldron design, these map to: direction control targets a person, the tug-of-war bar is always legible, Hex reversal enables comebacks, and ingredient choices feel reactive rather than aggressive ("I had to Shield!").
- For real-time shared-keyboard games, cross-player reactions work through reading shared mutable state (like the direction bar), not through direct move-by-move reaction timing. Players respond to the game state, not to individual opponent keypresses — this is the same pattern as the shared pile in Spit/Speed.
- In competitive real-time games, ingredient/action trade-offs must be conditional on game state, not fixed. "This ingredient always adds 3 pressure" has no trade-off. "This ingredient adds 3 pressure but is suicidal when direction is already toward you" creates a genuine decision. Every option should be the best choice in some game state and the worst choice in another.
- Sequential key combos (press 1 then 2) are technically reliable on shared keyboards; simultaneous combos (hold 1+2) are not, due to keyboard ghosting and phantom keyup events from the other player.
- The sweet spot for pick-up-and-play ingredient count is 3-4 distinct options. Beyond that, players can't form intuitions fast enough in a hackathon demo context. 4 keys per player (P1: 1234, P2: 7890) gives enough design space without cognitive overload.
